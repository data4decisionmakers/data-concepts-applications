16  Modernising Case Work in the Poverty Alleviation Unit

16.1 Case Overview

Marie has spent 30 years serving as a government social worker and now leads the Poverty Alleviation Unit as its Director. Over the course of her career, she has witnessed a steady rise in the number of families seeking welfare support. The demand on the unit has grown more complex over time, with more households presenting multiple and overlapping needs, including food insecurity, unemployment, unstable housing, health challenges, and school-related vulnerability.

The unit has built a strong institutional memory and maintains complete records of the families it has supported over the years. However, these records exist only on paper. Case files are stored in physical cabinets, archived in boxes, and accessed manually by staff who often rely on memory, file labels, and experience to retrieve information. This has allowed the unit to function, but it has also limited the ability of leadership to analyze trends, assess demand patterns, and proactively plan services.

Marie believes the unit now needs to move beyond reactive paper-based case work and begin using its data more strategically. She wants to better understand the families it serves, identify patterns in need, and improve the way welfare support is targeted and delivered. At the same time, she recognizes that her staff are largely traditional in their working methods and many are older professionals who have spent their careers managing cases on paper. They are experienced, committed, and deeply knowledgeable, but they may be hesitant to shift to digital systems and new ways of working.

This case asks participants to develop a strategy that will help Marie transform the Poverty Alleviation Unit into a more insight-driven, efficient, and client-centred organization while respecting the experience and concerns of its workforce.

16.2 Organizational Background

The Poverty Alleviation Unit is a public-sector service unit responsible for identifying vulnerable households, assessing social and economic need, coordinating welfare assistance, and maintaining records of support interventions. Its work is highly people-centred and often sensitive, involving confidential personal information and complex family circumstances. Staff members may conduct home visits, receive walk-in requests, coordinate referrals, review eligibility, and track follow-up actions over time.

The unit has historically operated through a paper-based workflow. Case notes, application forms, assessment reports, referral letters, follow-up records, and approval documents are all maintained in physical files. This has created a detailed archive of family histories, but one that is difficult to search, analyze, and update at scale.

The unit’s long-standing practices have helped preserve continuity, especially in an environment where trust, discretion, and personal relationships matter. Many staff members have developed strong expertise through years of direct case work. However, as demand has increased, these traditional methods are becoming harder to sustain. The unit now faces pressure to improve efficiency, strengthen reporting, and make better use of the information it already holds.

16.3 Marie’s Role

Marie is a respected public servant who has worked in the Poverty Alleviation Unit for three decades. She began her career as a frontline social worker and has gradually taken on more responsibility, developing a deep understanding of the families served by the unit as well as the operational realities of public welfare administration.

As Director, Marie is now responsible for setting the tone for modernization. She is not driven by technology for its own sake. Instead, she wants to create a system that helps the unit serve families more effectively, identify needs earlier, and make better decisions about resource allocation and intervention design.

Marie’s credibility comes from experience, empathy, and familiarity with the realities of field work. She understands that transformation will only succeed if it is practical, respectful of existing expertise, and aligned with the mission of the unit. She is aware that introducing digital case work will require more than new software. It will require changes in process, behaviour, training, governance, and leadership.

16.4 Current Operating Challenges

16.4.1 Paper-Based Case Records

The unit holds a rich archive of family records, but the information is dispersed across folders, filing cabinets, and storage rooms. Staff must manually search for files, compare handwritten notes, and piece together client histories from multiple documents. This makes it difficult to respond quickly to inquiries or review past support arrangements.

The paper-based system also creates several operational problems:

  • retrieving information is time-consuming;
  • files can be incomplete, misfiled, or damaged;
  • duplicate records may exist for the same family;
  • trends are difficult to identify without manual review;
  • and data sharing across teams is limited.

Although the paper records contain valuable information, they are underused as a strategic asset.

16.4.2 Rising Demand and Case Complexity

Marie has observed a steady increase in the number of families requiring support. This may reflect broader economic pressures, changes in household structure, rising living costs, or a greater awareness of available welfare services. Whatever the cause, the result is clear: more families are coming to the unit, and many are presenting more complicated and long-term needs than before.

The unit’s staff now face greater caseload pressure, more follow-ups, and more coordination with external partners such as schools, health services, and community organizations. As demand rises, the limitations of paper systems become more visible. Staff spend more time searching for information and less time analysing need or planning interventions.

16.4.3 Traditional Staff Culture

The unit’s workforce is experienced and service-oriented, but many staff members are comfortable with the paper methods they have used for years. Their reluctance to shift to digital case work is not necessarily resistance to improvement. In many cases, it reflects legitimate concerns:

  • fear of losing familiar routines,
  • concern about the learning curve,
  • worry that digital systems may slow them down,
  • uncertainty about confidentiality and data security,
  • and scepticism that new tools will actually help frontline work.

This cultural factor is one of Marie’s biggest challenges. A technology rollout that ignores staff sentiment may fail even if the tools themselves are sound.

16.5 Strategic Problem Statement

The Poverty Alleviation Unit has a valuable but underutilized archive of client information. The challenge is to transform that paper-based record system into a more useful source of insight and operational decision-making, while maintaining trust, protecting confidentiality, and supporting staff through the transition to digital case management.

Marie’s strategic question is how to modernize case work in a way that improves service quality and organizational intelligence without undermining staff confidence or the unit’s client-centered culture.

16.6 Why the Current Model Is Becoming Unsustainable

The current operating model has several long-term risks.

First, it limits the unit’s ability to understand who is using services, how needs are changing, and what types of interventions are most effective. Without structured data, decision-making relies too heavily on anecdote and memory.

Second, it makes planning difficult. Leadership cannot easily estimate demand by geography, household type, age group, referral source, or severity of need. That weakens the unit’s ability to advocate for resources or design targeted programs.

Third, it creates administrative inefficiency. Staff time is consumed by filing, searching, filing again, and reconstructing information rather than focusing on assessment and support.

Fourth, it exposes the unit to continuity risks. Paper files are vulnerable to physical damage, loss, duplication, and inconsistent storage practices.

Finally, it constrains the unit’s ability to collaborate with other agencies. If the unit cannot easily summarize caseloads or produce timely insights, it becomes harder to demonstrate impact or coordinate with partners.

16.7 Opportunities for Improvement

Marie sees three major opportunities.

16.7.1 1. Create a usable digital record of client history

The unit can begin capturing case information in a structured digital format, either by converting paper records into a searchable database or by starting with new cases and gradually digitizing historical files. This would allow staff to search records more efficiently, retrieve family histories faster, and reduce duplication.

16.7.2 2. Use data to understand client needs more deeply

Once records are digitized and structured, the unit could analyze patterns such as repeat support requests, common referral pathways, geographic concentrations of need, seasonal demand changes, and the types of assistance most frequently required. This would help Marie understand the client population more clearly and design better interventions.

16.7.3 3. Improve service coordination and planning

With better information, the unit could identify service gaps earlier, prioritize high-need households more effectively, and strengthen coordination with external agencies. Over time, the data could help leadership move from reactive case handling to more preventive and targeted support.

16.8 Operating Constraints

Any strategy must account for the realities of the unit’s environment.

The first constraint is confidentiality. Social welfare records contain highly sensitive personal and family information. Any digital solution must protect privacy, control access carefully, and comply with public-sector record keeping standards.

The second constraint is staff readiness. A significant portion of the team may not be comfortable with digital tools or may need substantial support to change established habits.

The third constraint is resource availability. Public-sector budgets are often limited, which means Marie may need to phase implementation and make a strong case for investment.

The fourth constraint is data quality. Paper files may contain inconsistencies, missing fields, or handwritten notes that are difficult to standardize. A digitization process will need clear rules for classification and data cleaning.

The fifth constraint is continuity of service. Families cannot be disrupted while the unit transitions to a new system. Case work must continue throughout the change process.

16.9 Stakeholder Landscape

A successful strategy will require support from several stakeholder groups.

Marie and senior leadership want better visibility into caseloads, stronger planning tools, and a more responsive service model.

Frontline social workers want a system that helps them manage cases efficiently without undermining the personal nature of their work.

Administrative staff need simple, reliable processes for file management, data entry, and retrieval.

IT or digital support teams may need to assist with system design, security, and implementation.

Government decision-makers will likely want evidence that the modernization effort improves efficiency, accountability, and outcomes.

Partner agencies such as schools, health providers, and community organizations may benefit from better coordination and clearer referral processes.

Client families need a service that remains respectful, confidential, and accessible during and after the transition.

16.10 Marie’s Vision

Marie wants the Poverty Alleviation Unit to become more than a repository of paper files. She wants it to become a learning organization that uses its own history to better understand the present and plan for the future.

Her vision includes:

  1. A digitized and searchable case record system that reduces time spent retrieving files and improves continuity of care.
  2. A better understanding of client needs and trends through structured data and reporting.
  3. A workforce that remains experienced and human-centred, but is supported by modern tools that make case work easier and more effective.
  4. Stronger planning and targeting of welfare support, based on real evidence rather than only historical habit or intuition.

Marie does not want technology to replace judgement, empathy, or professional discretion. She wants it to strengthen them.

16.11 Expectations for the Strategy Exercise

Participants should assume that Marie has leadership support but must build a practical and phased strategy. The task is not simply to digitize every file at once. The challenge is to design a realistic transformation that can be implemented over time.

A strong strategy is likely to address:

  • a baseline assessment of the current paper archive and workflows;
  • a method for digitizing or structuring client records;
  • a classification framework for key client and case data;
  • a staff engagement and training plan;
  • confidentiality and data governance requirements;
  • a pilot approach that reduces risk and demonstrates value;
  • and a way to use data insights for better service planning.

The strategy should also include measures of success such as retrieval time, completeness of records, staff adoption, reporting quality, and the ability to identify client needs more accurately.

16.12 Discussion Questions

  1. What should Marie prioritize in the first 90 days?
  2. Should the unit digitize all historical records, or begin with new cases first?
  3. How can Marie reduce resistance among older staff members?
  4. What types of client data would be most valuable to capture in a digital system?
  5. How can confidentiality and accessibility be balanced in a digital case work environment?
  6. What operational or social indicators should the unit use to understand client needs better?
  7. How can the unit use its records to improve planning without becoming overly bureaucratic?

16.13 Teaching Note

This case is designed to test participants’ ability to design a practical digital and organizational transition in a public-service setting. It highlights the tension between tradition and modernization, the importance of staff trust, and the value of turning existing records into decision-support assets.

Participants should avoid framing this as a pure IT implementation. The stronger approach is to treat it as a service redesign challenge involving people, process, data, governance, and change management.

16.14 Conclusion

The Poverty Alleviation Unit has years of accumulated experience and a complete historical record of the families it has supported. Yet most of that knowledge remains locked in paper files. Marie’s leadership presents an opportunity to convert that legacy into a more usable, insightful, and strategic asset.

The challenge is to modernize in a way that strengthens, rather than replaces, the values at the heart of the unit: compassion, confidentiality, dignity, and practical support for families in need. The strategy team’s task is to turn that vision into a realistic path forward.
